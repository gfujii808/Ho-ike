How the scan works Hōʻike reads the activity, matches Hawaiʻi-specific pathway logic, and surfaces local career connections.
What becomes visible The goal is not job-title matching. It is future legibility.
Brain drain Young, educated Hawaiʻi-born residents are disproportionately leaving.

DBEDT’s 2021 report describes recent population decline as driven in large part by net domestic outmigration and notes that outmigrants are more likely to be young and educated.

DBEDT report
Hidden curriculum Hōʻike targets informational frictions, not just job awareness.

Research frames the hidden curriculum as two frictions: knowing certain actions exist and believing those actions are worth taking. That is why Hōʻike gives scripts, prompts, and next steps instead of only listing careers.

Hidden curriculum paper
Local futures Construction, ag-tech, and emerging tech all matter for local retention.

Hawaiʻi Business highlights major 2026 construction activity and growing attention to technology, while the governor’s office points to increased use of drones, field sensors, GPS livestock tracking, and other farm technologies.

Economic outlook Ag-tech update
Decision support Students need support before the moment of choice, not only after they pick a pathway.

JFF argues that career planning is not linear and that young people need information, experiences, and advice before high-stakes decisions. ACT finds students value multiple paths, flexibility, and stronger fit between interests, values, and future choices.

JFF guide ACT brief JFF nondegree survey Harvard workforce note
