# Hōʻike 3-Minute Finalist Script

## 0:00-0:30 | The Problem

In Hawaiʻi, too many students can picture mainland opportunity more clearly than local opportunity. That is part of the brain-drain problem, but it is also a hidden-curriculum problem. Students are told to explore pathways, build networks, and think about the future without always being shown how their current schoolwork connects to a future here at home.

## 0:30-0:55 | The Product

Hōʻike Explorer is a Codex-built browser prototype for HIDOE students. It lives where students already work. A student pastes in an assignment title, and Hōʻike translates it into a `Professional Why`, Hawaiʻi pathway exploration, a local professional to learn from, and realistic next steps. We built it for Hawaiʻi first, but the underlying model can extend to other underserved communities facing similar hidden-curriculum barriers.

## 0:55-1:45 | The Live Translation Demo

I am going to paste in `Marine biology lab - reef health indicators`.

When I click `Translate`, Hōʻike instantly turns that assignment into a story. It identifies signals like marine science, field data, and care for place. Then it maps those signals to a local growth sector and explains why this work matters in Hawaiʻi.

The point is not just to turn homework into career prep. Hōʻike translates the assignment into strengths, interests, and ways a student might contribute to their community. In the demo, I also expose the `Hōʻike scan log` so judges can see the Codex-powered reasoning, not just the final interface.

## 1:45-2:20 | Local Voices and Safer Networking

Next, Hōʻike surfaces `Local Voices`. Instead of telling students to just network, it introduces a real local professional in a protected, school-safe format. Students can see what that person does, how they got there, what they love about the work, and what they wish they knew in high school.

Then Hōʻike offers guided `Talk Story` prompts like `What made you choose this work?` or `What should I try in high school if I am curious about this field?` That matters because the problem is not only access to jobs. It is also informational frictions around knowing what to ask, who to ask, and how to take a first step.

## 2:20-2:45 | Why This Is Useful

This matters because students do not always need certainty. They need exposure, language, and a safer first step. Hōʻike makes local futures visible before leaving starts to feel like the only ambitious option.

The student experience is also pseudonymous by default, with classroom-safe handles like `CuriousPueo` or `SunnyMango`, so exploration feels lower-stakes while schools can still preserve accountability. And the `Small Steps` section moves students from abstraction to preparation through reachable actions they can do now, this week, and next.

## 2:45-3:00 | Close

I built this prototype with Codex so I could move quickly on the product logic and focus on the cultural specifics of Hawaiʻi. Hōʻike helps students see that a future here at home can feel visible, ambitious, and actionable.

We also mocked the Canvas extension form factor to show a credible deployment surface for the same workflow, mounted in a Shadow DOM so the Hōʻike sidebar can live beside schoolwork without host-page CSS breaking the experience.
