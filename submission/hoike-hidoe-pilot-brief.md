# Hōʻike HIDOE Pilot Brief

## What This Could Become

Hōʻike is designed not only as a competition prototype, but as a credible starting point for a school-safe pilot in Hawaiʻi public schools. The product aim is simple: help students see how assignments, hobbies, community roles, and interests can connect to local futures they can contribute to here in Hawaiʻi.

## The Problem

Many students are expected to navigate the hidden curriculum of careers without being taught the scripts: how to interpret pathways, how to connect what they already do to local opportunity, and how to ask first questions without social risk. Those informational frictions can make local futures feel harder to imagine than leaving.

## The Intervention Logic

Hōʻike works inside a familiar student workflow and translates what a student already does into:

- a `Professional Why`
- Hawaiʻi-rooted pathway options
- school-safe `Local Voices`
- guided question starters
- concrete `Small Steps`

The core design goal is not to pressure students into choosing a job title early. It is to make local possibility more visible, more attainable, and easier to act on in low-stakes ways.

## Why This Fits A Pilot

- It is pseudonymous by default.
- It minimizes student data collection.
- It keeps professional discovery school-safe and moderated.
- It gives teachers and counselors classroom-level visibility without exposing students publicly.
- It is lightweight enough to test without requiring full district integration on day one.

## Example Pilot Shape

One practical first pilot could involve:

- 2 to 5 middle or high school classrooms, advisories, or pathway programs
- a teacher-managed class alias such as `PueoPod` or `HonuHui`
- student use during regular classroom or advisory time
- a moderated pool of staff-reviewed `Local Voices`, with simulated examples where needed
- a short feedback cycle with teachers, counselors, and students

## What To Measure

The pilot does not need heavy data collection to be useful. A first round could look at:

- how often students save pathways
- how often students draft or submit guided questions
- how often students complete `Small Steps`
- whether students can name more local futures after using the tool
- whether teachers observe more confidence, curiosity, or specificity in pathway discussions

## Research Opportunity

Hōʻike can also be framed as a research opportunity: a lightweight intervention against hidden-curriculum barriers and informational frictions in college-and-career exploration. A study could examine whether pseudonymous, school-safe pathway exploration increases local-future visibility, question confidence, and engagement for students who might otherwise opt out of career navigation early.

## Why HIDOE Conversations Matter

Hōʻike is strongest when framed as support for local belonging, contribution, and resilience. The goal is not only employability. It is helping students see that staying, returning, or contributing at home can also look ambitious.

## Near-Term Next Step

The most credible next step after the competition is a small, counselor- or teacher-supported pilot conversation with HIDOE-aligned educators, pathway leaders, or community partners to test whether the current workflow feels useful, safe, and culturally grounded in practice.

## Longer-Timeline Use

If the competition phase succeeds, the current demo and mockup system can become the basis for a late Fall or Winter 2026 research or pilot package. At that stage, the strongest next layer would likely be a cleaner educator-facing deck, a refined pilot protocol, and a tighter evaluation model rather than a broad new feature push.
